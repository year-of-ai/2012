# Facebook Initial Public Offering 2012

**Category**: Society & Economics
**Key figures**: Mark Zuckerberg (founder and CEO), Sheryl Sandberg (COO), David Ebersman (CFO), James Gorman (Morgan Stanley CEO), NASDAQ

## Background

When Facebook filed its S-1 registration statement with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission on February 1, 2012, it disclosed that the company had **845 million monthly active users** and had generated $3.71 billion in revenue in 2011 with net income of $1 billion. The filing kicked off months of intense anticipation — Facebook was then the world's dominant social network and one of the fastest-growing advertising platforms in history.

Facebook's IPO was the culmination of a years-long build-up. In 2004, Peter Thiel made a $500,000 angel investment for roughly 10% of the company. By 2011, secondary-market shares were trading at valuations implying a company worth $50–70 billion, and the SEC's rule requiring public disclosure for companies with more than 500 shareholders was widely cited as a factor pushing Facebook toward a formal public offering. Facebook had previously acquired Instagram on April 9, 2012 — just weeks before the IPO — for approximately $1 billion, a deal seen as a strategic move to absorb a fast-growing competitor before going public. See [Instagram–Facebook Acquisition (2012)](../science-technology/instagram-facebook-acquisition-2012.md).

## The IPO: May 18, 2012

Facebook priced its shares at **$38 per share** on the evening of May 17, 2012, the top of its revised range ($34–$38; the range had initially been $28–$35, raised twice during the roadshow). The offering included **421.2 million shares**, raising **$16 billion** — the largest technology IPO in U.S. history at that time, surpassing Google's $1.9 billion IPO in 2004 and dwarfing LinkedIn's $353 million offering in 2011. The total valuation of approximately **$104 billion** was also the highest in history for a newly public U.S. company.

Lead underwriters were **Morgan Stanley**, **JPMorgan Chase**, and **Goldman Sachs**, with 33 banks total in the underwriting syndicate. Mark Zuckerberg rang the opening bell remotely from Facebook's Menlo Park headquarters in a live video feed — an unusual move; he had declined to come to New York in person.

### The Opening Day Technical Failure

Trading was scheduled to begin at 11:00 a.m. Eastern Time but was delayed until **11:30 a.m.** due to technical problems with NASDAQ's matching engine. The source of the failure was a software flaw in NASDAQ's IPO auction system: a validation loop executed repeatedly when orders were modified, preventing the system from confirming trade executions. The result was that thousands of buy and sell orders submitted between 11:11 a.m. and 11:30 a.m. were neither confirmed nor cancelled for hours.

The stock opened at **$42.05** (above the $38 IPO price), but selling pressure was immediate and heavy. Morgan Stanley's stabilization desk purchased shares throughout the day to prevent a break below the $38 offering price. Facebook closed its first day at **$38.23**, a gain of just $0.23 (0.6%) — one of the weakest first-day performances for a high-profile tech IPO. For context, Google gained 18% on its first day; LinkedIn gained 109%.

NASDAQ ultimately paid **$62 million** in voluntary compensation to broker-dealers whose clients suffered losses due to the technical failure — far short of the estimated hundreds of millions in investor losses. The SEC and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) launched investigations.

## Post-IPO Collapse

The stock's decline after the IPO was steep and sustained:

| Date | Facebook Stock Price |
|------|---------------------|
| May 18, 2012 (IPO day close) | $38.23 |
| May 21, 2012 | $34.03 |
| June 6, 2012 | $26.90 |
| September 4, 2012 | $17.73 (52-week low) |
| August 6, 2013 | $38.05 (return to IPO price) |

The stock did not sustainably trade above the $38 IPO price until **August 2013** — approximately 15 months after the offering. By September 2012, its market capitalization had fallen from $104 billion to roughly $38 billion, a loss of more than $60 billion in value, wiping out tens of billions in paper wealth for early employees and investors who had held shares through the lock-up expiration.

Bloomberg News estimated that retail investors collectively lost approximately **$630 million** during the opening trading period due to the NASDAQ technical failure and resulting uncertainty about order execution.

## The Morgan Stanley Revenue Downgrade Controversy

The most damaging allegation to emerge from the IPO was that Morgan Stanley's research analysts had reduced their revenue projections for Facebook during the roadshow — responding to Facebook's May 9, 2012 amended S-1 filing acknowledging weaker-than-expected mobile advertising revenue growth — and had communicated those revisions selectively to certain institutional investors but not to retail investors.

The SEC and the Massachusetts Secretary of State both investigated. In December 2012, Morgan Stanley settled with Massachusetts regulators for **$5 million** without admitting wrongdoing. The case spotlighted the longstanding conflict between investment banks' underwriting and research functions and raised questions about information asymmetry in large IPOs.

Facebook CFO David Ebersman, who had led the decision to increase both the offering size (by 25%, adding 84 million shares) and the price range, became an internal focus of criticism. He later stated in interviews that increasing the offer at the top of the demand curve was a rational decision given the book demand they observed, and that the subsequent collapse reflected market sentiment, not underpricing.

## Regulatory Aftermath

- **SEC penalty**: In May 2013, NASDAQ agreed to pay a **$10 million** penalty to settle SEC charges arising from its systems and decision-making failures during the IPO — the largest penalty ever levied against an exchange at the time. The SEC's order also found that NASDAQ had assumed an unauthorized short position of more than three million Facebook shares, which it later covered for a profit of roughly $10.8 million. This penalty was separate from the **$62 million** voluntary accommodation fund NASDAQ established for affected member firms.
- **Zuckerberg's voting control**: The IPO structure gave Zuckerberg's Class B shares 10 votes each, versus 1 vote for publicly traded Class A shares, allowing him to retain effective control of the company despite owning less than a majority of total shares — a dual-class structure that became a model for subsequent tech IPOs (Snap, Lyft, Airbnb).

## Context and Broader Significance

The Facebook IPO occurred at a moment of heightened skepticism about financial markets. The Occupy Wall Street movement — which had drawn global attention from September 2011 through 2012 — had focused public anger on financial inequality and Wall Street's practices. See [Occupy Wall Street (2012)](occupy-wall-street-2012.md). The mismanagement of the Facebook IPO fed into a broader narrative of Wall Street benefiting institutional clients at retail investors' expense.

The IPO also arrived during the ongoing European debt crisis, at a moment when investor appetite for risk assets was periodically disrupted by fears of sovereign defaults and eurozone fragmentation. See [European Debt Crisis (2012)](european-debt-crisis-2012.md). Facebook's post-IPO collapse occurred partly in this volatile macro-market context.

Despite the disastrous IPO, Facebook recovered decisively once it demonstrated a viable mobile advertising model. By Q3 2013, mobile advertising represented 49% of total ad revenue. By 2021, Facebook (renamed Meta Platforms) had a market capitalization exceeding $1 trillion — roughly ten times its IPO value. The IPO remains a canonical business school case study in overpricing, underwriter conflicts, exchange technical failures, and the long-term resilience of platform monopolies.
