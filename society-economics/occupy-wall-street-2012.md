# Occupy Wall Street — 2012 Momentum

**Category**: Society & Economics  
**Key figures**: Kalle Lasn and Micah White (Adbusters founders, credited with originating the call to occupy); David Graeber (anthropologist and early organizer); NYC General Assembly leadership; labor unions co-organizing the May Day action

## Summary

The Occupy Wall Street movement, which began on September 17, 2011 with an encampment in Zuccotti Park in Manhattan's Financial District, entered its second year in 2012 with renewed efforts to sustain momentum and expand its reach. After New York City police cleared the Zuccotti Park encampment on November 15, 2011 — an action replicated across dozens of cities in a nationwide coordinated crackdown — the movement shifted toward mass coordinated actions and commemorative demonstrations rather than permanent physical occupation.

Throughout 2012, OWS organized multiple high-profile actions against a backdrop of a U.S. presidential election year that made economic inequality a prominent national debate. A March 17 attempt to reoccupy Zuccotti Park on the movement's six-month anniversary drew hundreds of participants; police made over 70 arrests. The movement's signature 2012 action came on **May 1 (International Workers' Day)**, when a citywide general strike and day of action brought an estimated 30,000 to 50,000 marchers through New York City streets, coordinated with simultaneous rallies in Los Angeles, Chicago, Seattle, San Francisco, and Washington D.C.

The September 17 one-year anniversary demonstration saw returning activists attempt to block access to the New York Stock Exchange and key surrounding intersections, resulting in **185 arrests** as police deployed in force to prevent disruption to financial operations.

## The May Day General Strike

The May 1, 2012 action was the most ambitious organizational effort in OWS's history. Conceived as a general strike in the tradition of International Workers' Day labor mobilizations, it brought together Occupy organizers with labor unions — including segments of the Service Employees International Union (SEIU) — immigrant-rights groups, and student organizations. In New York City, the march proceeded from Bryant Park through Midtown Manhattan to Union Square and ultimately toward the Financial District.

The day's actions spread across at least 115 cities in the United States, according to organizer accounts, and parallel demonstrations occurred in dozens of countries. The breadth of the May Day action demonstrated that Occupy's horizontal organizing model could coordinate geographically dispersed participation, even in the absence of the centralized encampments that had originally defined the movement.

## Political Context: The 2012 Election Year

OWS's 2012 momentum unfolded against the backdrop of the [2012 U.S. Presidential Election](../history-politics/2012-us-presidential-election.md) — a campaign in which income inequality and financial regulation featured more prominently than in any election since the New Deal era. The movement's core vocabulary — particularly the **"We are the 99%"** framing popularized by the movement, building on the original call to occupy issued by the Canadian activist magazine *Adbusters* — migrated into mainstream political discourse. Democratic candidates, including incumbent President [Barack Obama](../people/barack-obama-2012-reelection.md), began referencing income inequality more explicitly, while Republican challengers criticized the movement as anti-capitalist.

Independent analyses of 2012 campaign speech patterns found measurable increases in references to inequality, the middle class, and corporate accountability compared with the 2008 election cycle — a discursive shift that researchers at the Pew Research Center and Columbia University attributed at least partly to Occupy's agenda-setting effect.

The movement's influence was not without controversy: critics from across the political spectrum noted that OWS produced no formal policy platform, electoral candidates, or legislative victories, distinguishing it from earlier American protest movements that translated street mobilization into concrete institutional change.

## Economic Context

OWS's 2012 activity took place within a specific macroeconomic environment: U.S. unemployment stood at approximately **8.1%** in August 2012 (down from 9.0% in August 2011 but still historically elevated), and the share of national income accruing to the top 1% of earners had reached **22.5%** — the highest level since the eve of the Great Depression, according to research by economists Emmanuel Saez and Thomas Piketty published in 2012.

The same year, the [European Debt Crisis](european-debt-crisis-2012.md) was reshaping economic conditions across the Atlantic, reinforcing the global resonance of Occupy's critique of financial institutions and austerity. Occupy movements had emerged in Spain (the *Indignados*), Greece, the United Kingdom, and Germany, and the 2012 actions in the United States were partly synchronized with European counterparts who shared the critique of economic inequality driven by deregulated financial systems.

## Organizational Structure and Internal Tensions

OWS operated through a **General Assembly** (GA) model that required consensus decision-making, which was both a philosophical statement and a practical challenge. By 2012, internal tensions between advocates of decentralized horizontalism and those seeking more structured leadership had fragmented local chapters in several cities. The loss of physical encampments removed a shared focal point and forced organizers to adapt to a dispersed, network-based model.

Splinter groups pursued more targeted campaigns: **Strike Debt** (launched in 2012) focused on abolishing predatory student and medical debt, the affiliated **Rolling Jubilee** campaign, launched in November 2012, went on to buy and cancel roughly $15 million in medical debt, and **Occupy Sandy** mobilized thousands of volunteers for hurricane relief in October–November 2012 after [Hurricane Sandy](hurricane-sandy-2012.md) struck the New York region — demonstrating the movement's capacity to redirect its organizational infrastructure toward mutual aid.

## Legacy and Assessment

Although Occupy Wall Street did not establish a permanent organizational base, its 2012 momentum represented the final major surge of nationally coordinated Occupy action. After 2012, participation declined sharply, and the movement fragmented into smaller, issue-specific activist groups focused on student debt, housing justice, and police accountability.

The movement's durable legacy lies in the cultural and discursive shift it catalyzed. The **"99%"** concept became a standard reference point in American economic journalism and political speech. Scholars including Zeynep Tufekci (*Twitter and Tear Gas*, 2017) and Manuel Castells (*Networks of Outrage and Hope*, 2012) analyzed OWS as a prototype of networked social movements enabled by social media — a form of mobilization that lowered barriers to participation while also producing organizational fragility.

OWS's 2012 activity belongs to a global wave of protest movements that defined the early 2010s, alongside the Arab Spring (2011–2012), the Spanish *Indignados*, and protests connected to the economic disruptions of the [European Debt Crisis](european-debt-crisis-2012.md).
