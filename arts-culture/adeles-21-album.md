# Adele's Album "21" — 2012 Dominance

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Adele Laurie Blue Adkins (artist); Paul Epworth (producer/co-writer); Rick Rubin (executive producer); Jonathan Dickins (manager); Columbia Records (US label)

## Background

Adele Laurie Blue Adkins, known professionally as Adele, was born on May 5, 1988, in Tottenham, north London. She had released her debut album *19* in 2008, which earned her early Grammy recognition (Best New Artist and Best Female Pop Vocal Performance at the 51st Grammy Awards), but it was her follow-up that transformed her into a global phenomenon.

*21* was recorded primarily between 2009 and 2010, in the aftermath of a failed romantic relationship. Adele wrote or co-wrote nine of the eleven tracks. Key sessions took place in London and Los Angeles, with producers including Paul Epworth (who co-wrote and produced "Rolling in the Deep" and "He Won't Go"), Ryan Tedder, Fraser T. Smith, and Dan Wilson. Rick Rubin served as executive producer and worked with Adele on the more stripped-back tracks. The album's title refers to the age at which Adele wrote and recorded most of the material.

*21* was released on **January 24, 2011**, in the United Kingdom and on **February 22, 2011**, in the United States. It entered the UK Albums Chart at number one and the US Billboard 200 at number one in its debut week.

## Commercial Performance

The album's commercial trajectory across 2011–2012 was without precedent in the SoundScan era (the period since Nielsen SoundScan began tracking US music sales in May 1991):

- **"Rolling in the Deep"** was released as the lead single in November 2010 and spent **seven consecutive weeks** at number 1 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 2011, selling over 8 million copies in the US alone.
- **"Someone Like You"**, released as the third single, also reached number 1 on the Billboard Hot 100 and number 1 in the UK, making Adele the first solo artist in the UK charts to have two singles simultaneously in the top 5.
- The album spent a cumulative **24 weeks at number 1** on the US Billboard 200 — the longest run for any album since Whitney Houston's *Whitney* in 1987 and the longest-ever run by a female solo artist.
- *21* was certified **Diamond** (10 million US copies) by the RIAA in **November 2012**, becoming one of only a handful of albums to reach this milestone.
- It was named the best-selling album on iTunes for both **2011 and 2012 consecutively**, a feat with no recorded precedent on the platform.
- *21* was the **best-selling album in the United States for two consecutive calendar years** — 2011 and 2012 — the first album to achieve this in the SoundScan era, outselling all new releases including major pop and hip-hop records.

By the end of 2012, global sales of *21* exceeded **30 million copies**, placing it among the best-selling albums of all time. As of 2024, it remains the best-selling album of the entire 21st century.

## The 54th Grammy Awards (February 12, 2012)

The defining institutional moment of *21*'s run came at the **54th Annual Grammy Awards**, held at the Staples Center in Los Angeles on **February 12, 2012**. Adele had been nominated in six categories:

| Category | Nominee | Result |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Album of the Year | *21* | **Won** |
| Record of the Year | "Rolling in the Deep" | **Won** |
| Song of the Year | "Rolling in the Deep" | **Won** |
| Best Pop Vocal Album | *21* | **Won** |
| Best Pop Solo Performance | "Someone Like You" | **Won** |
| Best Short Form Music Video | "Rolling in the Deep" | **Won** |

Adele swept all six categories, becoming the second woman in Grammy history to win six awards in a single night, matching Beyoncé's record set at the 2010 ceremony. The sweep was particularly notable because Adele had undergone **vocal cord microsurgery** on November 7, 2011 (just three months earlier), which had forced the cancellation of her North American tour. Her live performance of "Rolling in the Deep" at the Grammys — her first major public performance post-surgery — received widespread critical acclaim.

The Grammy broadcast reached an estimated **39.9 million viewers** in the United States, the most-watched Grammy telecast since 1984. In the week following the ceremony, album sales spiked approximately **200%**, with *21* selling approximately **730,000 copies** in that single week — one of the highest weekly tallies for any album in the SoundScan era outside of holiday release weeks.

## Cultural Impact

*21* represented a commercially and critically anomalous achievement: a breakup album rooted in classic soul and pop songwriting that outsold teen pop and hip-hop releases at the height of the digital singles era. Several dimensions of its impact are widely cited:

- **Genre crossover**: The album blended Motown-influenced soul ("Rolling in the Deep"), folk-influenced balladry ("Someone Like You"), country-tinged pop ("Turning Tables"), and gospel ("Take It All") into a cohesive commercial package that attracted listeners across demographic categories.
- **Streaming and download behavior**: *21* is often cited as an inflection point in album-vs.-singles behavior, as listeners purchased the full album rather than individual tracks, bucking the post-iTunes trend toward single-song purchases.
- **Influence on industry strategy**: The album's multi-year sales trajectory — unusual in an era of rapid release cycles — prompted music industry analysis suggesting that certain albums could sustain commercial momentum through touring and awards exposure rather than requiring immediate sequel releases.
- **Cultural touchstone**: In the broader context of 2012, "Someone Like You" became a ubiquitous cultural reference — used in advertising, film soundtracks, and public events — and Adele's vocal cord surgery and recovery became a widely followed public narrative.
