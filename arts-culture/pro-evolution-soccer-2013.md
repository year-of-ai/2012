# Pro Evolution Soccer 2013

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Cristiano Ronaldo (cover star); Shinji Kagawa (Japan cover); Shingo Takatsuka (game producer); Konami (developer/publisher)

## Summary

Pro Evolution Soccer 2013 (PES 2013), published by Konami, launched in 2012 as a landmark release in the long-running football simulation franchise. Regional release dates varied slightly: Australia received the game on September 20, 2012; European territories on September 21; and North America on September 25. The title shipped across multiple platforms — PlayStation 3, PlayStation 2, Xbox 360, Nintendo Wii, and PC — making it one of the broader multi-platform releases in the franchise's history. Cristiano Ronaldo of Real Madrid graced the European PlayStation 3 cover, while Manchester United midfielder Shinji Kagawa appeared on the Japanese edition for a second consecutive year.

PES 2013 marked a significant milestone for the franchise by including all twenty teams from Brazil's national Campeonato Brasileiro Série A for the first time, alongside comprehensive UEFA Champions League and UEFA Europa League licensing. The PlayStation 3 and Xbox 360 versions each earned a Metacritic score of 82 out of 100, signaling a strong critical recovery after several years of mixed reviews. The *Guardian* praised the game as a return to form, and one widely circulated verdict declared: "It might have taken a very long time but PES is finally good again." Japanese domestic sales for 2012 reached 399,340 copies, demonstrating solid commercial performance in the franchise's primary market.

## Development and Gameplay Mechanics

Central to PES 2013's design was the expanded **Full Control** system, which gave players greater precision over dribbling, shooting, and first-touch control than any previous entry in the series. The game introduced a refined **natural player movement** engine that aimed to replicate the body mechanics of real athletes during sprints, turns, and aerial challenges. Producer Shingo Takatsuka stated in pre-release interviews that the team had studied thousands of hours of match footage to improve animations — a methodology that contrasted with earlier iterative development cycles.

The **myClub** precursor concept — allowing players to build custom squads — was not yet present in PES 2013; that feature would arrive in later editions. However, the **Master League** career mode was deepened, and the online multiplayer infrastructure received stability improvements. PES 2013 also retained the beloved manual passing system that hardcore fans credited for the series' distinct tactical feel compared to rival FIFA.

The PC version shipped later (October 2012 in North America) and featured the same core gameplay but with a thriving community modding scene that extended the game's commercial lifespan well beyond what official sales figures captured.

## Cover Stars and Marketing

Cristiano Ronaldo's selection as the European cover star reflected his status as one of the world's most recognizable athletes following his 2011–12 season with Real Madrid, in which he scored 60 goals in all competitions. His presence lent the game visibility in both football markets and broader popular culture, fitting the wider 2012 explosion of sports media that also included the [London 2012 Olympics](../history-politics/london-2012-olympics.md) — arguably the most-watched sporting event of the calendar year.

Shinji Kagawa's Japanese cover appearance coincided with his high-profile move from Borussia Dortmund to Manchester United in the summer of 2012, making him one of the most covered players in Japanese sports media at the time.

## The PES vs. FIFA Rivalry

By 2012, Electronic Arts' FIFA franchise had eroded PES's once-dominant market share through superior licensing deals and iterative graphical improvements. FIFA 13, released in September 2012, sold approximately 4.5 million copies in its first week across all platforms — compared to PES 2013's more modest figures. However, PES 2013's strong critical reception narrowed the perceived quality gap, and the Konami title retained a loyal global fanbase, particularly in South America, the Middle East, and parts of Asia where FIFA's licensing premiums mattered less.

The rivalry between the two series mirrored broader dynamics in 2012 entertainment: dominant commercial incumbents facing resurgent challengers through product differentiation. This pattern appeared across film ([The Hobbit: An Unexpected Journey](the-hobbit-unexpected-journey.md) challenging blockbuster norms with its high-frame-rate experiment) and music ([Adele's *21*](adeles-21-album.md) sustaining its chart dominance into 2012).

## Commercial Performance and Platform Legacy

PES 2013 was the **final Pro Evolution Soccer title released for Nintendo's home consoles**, a decision that reflected the industry's consolidation around PlayStation and Xbox platforms. The Nintendo Wii version, released alongside the others in September 2012, sold modestly and was not carried forward into subsequent iterations of the franchise.

Beyond Japan's 399,340 copies, the game performed strongly in Italy, Spain, Argentina, and Brazil — markets with the deepest football gaming traditions. Total global sales across all platforms were not individually broken out by Konami but industry trackers estimated multi-million-unit performance when PC and legacy platforms were included.

## Significance

Pro Evolution Soccer 2013 represented a critical inflection point in the sports gaming industry. Its first-time inclusion of Brazilian league clubs, official UEFA tournament licensing, and the Full Control gameplay overhaul collectively restored critical credibility to a franchise that had lost ground to FIFA through the late 2000s. The 2012 release coincided with growing international recognition of Cristiano Ronaldo and reflected the global cultural prominence of European club football.

The game's platform choices — maintaining PS2 and Wii support while investing heavily in PS3/Xbox 360 quality — illustrated the transitional nature of gaming in 2012, a year before the PlayStation 4 and Xbox One would prompt the next generational shift. This transitional character is a recurring theme across 2012 arts and culture, where established forms found themselves simultaneously at a peak and on the cusp of disruption.
