# UEFA Euro 2012 Football Championship

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: Vicente del Bosque (Spain manager), Andrés Iniesta (tournament MVP), Cesare Prandelli (Italy manager), Fernando Torres (Golden Boot), Iker Casillas (Golden Glove)

## Summary

UEFA Euro 2012 was the fourteenth edition of the European Football Championship, held between June 8 and July 1, 2012, across eight venues in Poland and Ukraine. The tournament brought together 16 national teams competing across four groups, with 31 matches played in total. Spain emerged as champions, defeating Italy 4–0 in the final at the Olympiyskiy Stadium in Kyiv on July 1—the most emphatic margin of victory in European Championship final history.

The tournament marked the first time Poland and Ukraine co-hosted a major UEFA event, representing an unprecedented infrastructure investment in Eastern Europe. Five new stadiums were constructed or substantially redeveloped for the competition, including the National Stadium in Warsaw and the Metalist Stadium in Kharkiv. Spain's triumph made them the first national team in history to win three consecutive major international tournaments: UEFA Euro 2008, the 2010 FIFA World Cup, and UEFA Euro 2012.

## Tournament Structure and Group Stage

The 16 participating teams were divided into four groups of four, with the top two from each group advancing to the knockout rounds. Group C—often described as the "Group of Death"—contained Spain, Italy, Ireland, and Croatia, all of whom had realistic ambitions. Spain and Italy both advanced after drawing 1–1 in their group encounter, a match widely regarded as a tactical masterclass. The Republic of Ireland returned to the tournament after a 24-year absence but was eliminated in the group stage without winning a match. Ukraine, in their first major tournament as an independent nation, were controversially eliminated following disputed officiating decisions.

The Netherlands, pre-tournament favorites ranked among the world's top three and runners-up at the 2010 FIFA World Cup, suffered a historic group-stage collapse, losing all three of their matches—to Denmark (0–1), Germany (1–2), and Portugal (1–2)—to finish bottom of Group B.

## Knockout Rounds and Final

In the quarterfinals, Germany defeated Greece 4–2, while Spain beat France 2–0 and Portugal beat the Czech Republic 1–0. Italy eliminated England on penalties after a 0–0 draw, sending the English home in familiar fashion.

The semifinal between Spain and Portugal finished 0–0 after extra time; Spain advanced 4–2 on penalties. The other semifinal proved more dramatic: Mario Balotelli scored both goals as Italy defeated Germany 2–1, maintaining Italy's long unbeaten record against Germany at major tournaments.

The final on July 1 in Kyiv was a rout. David Silva opened the scoring in the 14th minute, Jordi Alba doubled the lead before half-time, Fernando Torres and Juan Mata added goals after the break. Italy, reduced to tactical confusion by Spain's relentless pressing and possession, were unable to register a shot on target in the second half. Andrés Iniesta was named Best Player of the Tournament, Iker Casillas won the Golden Glove for goalkeeping, and Fernando Torres took the Golden Boot—one of six players level on three goals, he won the award on the tiebreakers of an assist and the fewest minutes played.

## Attendance and Records

The championship set attendance records for the 16-team format, registering 1,440,896 total spectators across all 31 matches—an average of 46,481 per game. The highest single-match attendance was 63,170 at the Olympic Stadium in Kyiv for the final. These figures came despite economic strain in much of Europe; Ukraine in particular offered some of the tournament's most affordable ticketing, drawing large crowds of local supporters.

Six players shared the top scorer position with three goals each: Mario Balotelli, Mario Gómez, Cristiano Ronaldo, Alan Dzagoev, Fernando Torres, and Mario Mandžukić.

## Context: Economics, Politics, and Football

Euro 2012 unfolded against the backdrop of the ongoing [European Debt Crisis](../society-economics/european-debt-crisis-2012.md), which had particularly severe effects on Greece, Portugal, and Ireland—three of the tournament's participating nations. Protests in Athens periodically intersected with Greek national team travel and fan gatherings, underscoring the political tensions surrounding the tournament's host continent.

Ukraine's co-hosting was also accompanied by international scrutiny over the imprisonment of former Prime Minister Yulia Tymoshenko, with several European leaders declining to attend matches held in Ukraine as a form of protest. European Commission president José Manuel Barroso and German chancellor Angela Merkel were among those who attended only Polish venues.

The 2012 summer was a remarkable one for European sport: just weeks after the Euro final, London hosted the [2012 Summer Olympics](../history-politics/london-2012-olympics.md), and England celebrated the [Queen's Diamond Jubilee](../history-politics/queen-elizabeth-diamond-jubilee-2012.md) that same month.

## Legacy

Euro 2012 was the last European Championship to use the 16-team format that had been standard since 1996; UEFA Euro 2016 expanded to 24 teams. The 16-team era's final edition thus ended with perhaps its most definitive performance—Spain's 4–0 dismantling of Italy remains the largest winning margin in a European Championship final.

Spain's victory completed an unprecedented era of international dominance, their core built around Xavi Hernández, Andrés Iniesta, and a supporting cast of FC Barcelona and Real Madrid players who executed club-level possession football at international scale. No national team has since replicated a run of three consecutive major international titles.
