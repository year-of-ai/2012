# The Hobbit: An Unexpected Journey

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Peter Jackson (director); Martin Freeman (Bilbo Baggins); Ian McKellen (Gandalf); Richard Armitage (Thorin Oakenshield); Cate Blanchett (Galadriel); Ian Holm (elderly Bilbo); Andy Serkis (Gollum); Howard Shore (composer); Fran Walsh, Philippa Boyens, Guillermo del Toro (co-writers)

## Background and Production History

J.R.R. Tolkien published *The Hobbit, or There and Back Again* in **1937** as a children's novel set in the same fictional world as his later epic *The Lord of the Rings* (1954–1955). Peter Jackson had long planned a return to Middle-earth after completing his *The Lord of the Rings* trilogy (2001–2003), which collectively grossed approximately **$2.92 billion** worldwide and won **17 Academy Awards** including Best Picture for *The Return of the King* (2003).

Rights negotiations delayed the project for years. Mexican director **Guillermo del Toro** was originally announced as director in April 2008, with Jackson producing. After nearly two years of pre-production work — including extensive conceptual design — del Toro departed the project in **May 2010** due to protracted delays caused by MGM's financial difficulties. Jackson then stepped in as director, announcing in October 2010 that he would helm the films himself.

**Principal photography** began on **March 21, 2011**, in New Zealand — the same country that had hosted the LOTR shoots — and continued for approximately 266 shooting days across locations including Matamata (the Shire), the Remarkables mountain range, and purpose-built sets at Park Road Post Production in Wellington. The production employed thousands of New Zealand crew members and generated significant economic activity for the country.

## Cast

**Martin Freeman** was cast as the young Hobbit Bilbo Baggins, the unexpected hero who joins a company of thirteen dwarves led by Thorin Oakenshield (Richard Armitage) on a quest to reclaim the Lonely Mountain from the dragon Smaug. Freeman's casting was partly complicated by his commitments to the BBC series *Sherlock*; the productions had to coordinate schedules extensively. **Ian McKellen** reprised his role as the wizard Gandalf the Grey, and **Ian Holm** returned to briefly portray the elderly Bilbo in a framing sequence set during the events of *The Fellowship of the Ring*. **Cate Blanchett** returned as Galadriel in a sequence not present in Tolkien's original novel but adapted from the appendices of *The Return of the King*.

**Andy Serkis** reprised his motion-capture performance as Gollum, appearing in the pivotal "Riddles in the Dark" chapter — widely praised by critics as the film's highlight.

## Technical Innovation: 48 Frames Per Second

The most technically controversial aspect of the film was Jackson's decision to shoot and exhibit it in **High Frame Rate (HFR)** at **48 frames per second** — exactly double the 24 fps standard that had defined cinema since the late 1920s. Jackson argued that HFR would reduce motion blur and strobing artifacts in action sequences, producing a smoother, more detailed visual presentation.

The 48fps version required theaters to install upgraded digital projectors; approximately **450 theaters** worldwide were equipped to project it at HFR at launch. The majority of the **4,045 screens** that opened the film in North America projected it at standard 24fps in 2D or 3D.

Critical and audience reaction to the HFR presentation was sharply divided:
- Proponents described the image as clearer and more immersive during action.
- Critics argued the format created an uncanny "soap opera effect" that made the fictional world look too real, undermining the film's fantasy register.
- Some critics noted that the format exposed the artificiality of practical effects and makeup that would be less apparent at 24fps.

The HFR experiment was not widely adopted by mainstream studios after *The Hobbit* trilogy and has since remained a niche format, though it influenced later technical discussions about cinema standards, including Ang Lee's 120fps experiments with *Billy Lynn's Long Halftime Walk* (2016) and *Gemini Man* (2019).

## Box Office Performance

*An Unexpected Journey* opened in North America on **December 14, 2012**, and internationally on December 12–13 in most markets. Its financial performance:

| Market | Revenue |
|--------|---------|
| North American opening weekend | $84.6 million |
| North American total | $303 million |
| International total | $714 million |
| Worldwide total | **$1.017 billion** |

The film crossed the **$1 billion** mark, becoming the **fifteenth film in cinema history** to reach that milestone and the **fourth highest-grossing film of 2012**, behind *The Avengers* ($1.52 billion), *Skyfall* ($1.11 billion), and *The Dark Knight Rises* ($1.08 billion). All four top films were franchise sequels or extensions, reflecting the dominance of franchise cinema in 2012.

## Critical Reception

Critical reception was notably more mixed than the *Lord of the Rings* films:

- **Rotten Tomatoes**: 64% approval rating from 303 reviews as of the film's initial run (critics' consensus cited "slow pacing" and uneven tonal register, contrasting Middle-earth epic grandeur with lighter children's-book material)
- **Metacritic**: 58/100 (indicating "mixed or average reviews")
- **CinemaScore**: Audiences gave the film an **A−** grade, indicating stronger audience satisfaction than critical response

The most consistently praised element was Andy Serkis's motion-capture performance as Gollum. The least praised were the opening Shire sequences (70+ minutes of prologue before the quest begins) and the expanded material drawn from Tolkien's appendices, including a storyline featuring the Necromancer (later revealed as Sauron) that had no equivalent in *The Hobbit* novel.

## Academy Awards

At the **85th Academy Awards** (February 24, 2013), *An Unexpected Journey* received three nominations but won none:

- Best Production Design (nominees: Ra Vincent, Dan Hennah)
- Best Makeup and Hairstyling (nominees: Peter King, Rick Findlater, Tami Lane)
- Best Visual Effects (nominees: Joe Letteri, Eric Saindon, David Clayton, R. Christopher White)

The film later received the **Academy Scientific and Technical Award** for advancements in motion-capture character animation technology, recognizing the underlying technology developed across the *Lord of the Rings* and *Hobbit* productions.

## Sequels and Trilogy

*An Unexpected Journey* was the first of three films adapting Tolkien's novel:
- *The Hobbit: The Desolation of Smaug* — December 13, 2013 ($958 million worldwide)
- *The Hobbit: The Battle of the Five Armies* — December 17, 2014 ($956 million worldwide)

The three films collectively grossed approximately **$2.93 billion** worldwide, nearly matching the original trilogy's total despite more mixed critical reception. Howard Shore's musical score for all three films incorporated leitmotifs developed for the original trilogy while introducing new themes for the Dwarf company.
