# Kim Jong-un's 2012 Leadership Consolidation in North Korea

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Kim Jong-un (Supreme Leader), Kim Jong-il (father, died December 17, 2011), Kim Il-sung (grandfather, centennial April 15, 2012), Ri Yong-ho (Chief of the General Staff, purged July 2012), Kim Kyong-hui (aunt, regent figure), Jang Song-thaek (uncle by marriage, regent until purge December 2013), Choe Ryong-hae (political director, Korean People's Army)

## Summary

Kim Jong-un entered 2012 as the formally installed Supreme Leader of North Korea following his father Kim Jong-il's sudden death on December 17, 2011. He was believed to be 28 years old — having been born either in 1983 or 1984; North Korea did not officially disclose his birth year during the succession period. His ascension was announced on December 19, 2011, via North Korean state television, and within days he had been designated Supreme Commander of the Korean People's Army, the highest military title in the DPRK.

The year 2012 was dominated by three overlapping tasks: staging a visible demonstration of legitimacy on the centennial of his grandfather Kim Il-sung's birth (April 15), pushing forward with a military satellite launch to underscore technological ambitions, and reshuffling the senior military officer corps to install loyalists. By December 2012, a second rocket launch — this time successful — signaled North Korea's growing ballistic missile capability and placed Kim Jong-un's regime on a collision course with the international community.

## The April 15 Centennial Parade

April 15, 2012 — the 100th anniversary of Kim Il-sung's birth — was designated "Day of the Sun," North Korea's most important national holiday. A massive military parade through Kim Il-sung Square in Pyongyang served as Kim Jong-un's first significant set-piece moment before the North Korean public and international observers. An estimated 100,000 people attended or participated. The parade featured new ballistic missiles carried on Chinese-made transporter-erector-launchers (TELs), including what analysts later identified as the KN-08 intercontinental ballistic missile (ICBM), displayed publicly for the first time. Kim Jong-un delivered his first public speech as leader, a roughly 20-minute address broadcast on state television — his father Kim Jong-il had never delivered a public speech during his 17-year rule.

## The April 13 Rocket Launch Failure

Two days before the centennial parade, on April 13, North Korea launched the Unha-3 rocket from the Sohae Satellite Launching Station, carrying what the regime described as the Kwangmyongsong-3 earth observation satellite. The rocket broke apart approximately 90 seconds after launch, with debris falling into the Yellow Sea. The launch was publicly announced in advance — an unusual step for North Korea — and foreign journalists were allowed to tour the launch facility beforehand, suggesting the regime expected success and wanted the propaganda benefit of live coverage.

The failure was an embarrassment timed immediately before the centennial. The United States, South Korea, Japan, and the European Union condemned the launch as a violation of UN Security Council Resolution 1874 (2009), which barred North Korea from ballistic missile testing. The UN Security Council issued a chairman's statement of condemnation. Despite the failure, North Korea pressed forward: on December 12, 2012, the Unha-3 rocket successfully placed the Kwangmyongsong-3 Unit 2 satellite in orbit, making North Korea the tenth nation to independently launch a satellite.

## Military Purges and Personnel Changes

Kim Jong-un's authority over the military required rapid, visible assertion. In July 2012, General Ri Yong-ho, Chief of the General Staff and one of the most powerful military figures inherited from the Kim Jong-il era, was abruptly removed from all positions, reportedly citing health reasons — a standard North Korean euphemism for political removal. Ri had been a prominent figure at Kim Jong-il's funeral and a member of the standing committee of the Politburo; his removal signaled that Kim Jong-un would not be constrained by the senior officer corps.

Choe Ryong-hae, a political officer rather than a career military commander, was elevated to Vice Marshal and Director of the Korean People's Army's General Political Bureau, replacing more traditional military figures with loyalists tied to the ruling party's organizational structures rather than the military-first (Songun) apparatus that had dominated under Kim Jong-il.

## Economic and Ideological Signaling

Alongside military consolidation, Kim Jong-un made symbolic economic gestures in 2012. He visited factories, farms, and amusement parks in Pyongyang, including the Rungna People's Pleasure Ground opened in July 2012. These visits were extensively covered by state media and contrasted visually with Kim Jong-il's more reclusive governing style. On April 11, the Fourth Conference of the Workers' Party of Korea elected Kim Jong-un to the newly created post of First Secretary, formalizing his leadership of the party. Two days later, on April 13 — the same day as the rocket launch — the Supreme People's Assembly revised the constitution, naming him First Chairman of the National Defence Commission; its revised preamble described North Korea as a nuclear-armed state, effectively constitutionalizing the country's nuclear-weapons status.

## International Context

Kim Jong-un's consolidation unfolded within a year of significant global leadership transitions. In Russia, [Vladimir Putin returned to the presidency in May 2012](vladimir-putin-2012-presidency.md) for a third presidential term after a managed transition. In Egypt, [Mohamed Morsi of the Muslim Brotherhood was elected president](mohamed-morsi-2012-presidency.md) in June 2012. These parallel leadership transitions highlighted 2012 as a year of consequential political change across authoritarian and semi-authoritarian systems. North Korea remained the most opaque, with no independent journalism or external verification of internal events.

## Significance and Legacy

Kim Jong-un's 2012 consolidation established the fundamental structure of his rule that would persist for more than a decade. His willingness to publicly remove senior generals — beginning with Ri Yong-ho — broke with the implicit bargain between the Kim family and the military that had underpinned the Songun era. His uncle Jang Song-thaek, who served as a critical regent figure through 2013, was publicly executed in December 2013, demonstrating that even the innermost familial circle was not immune.

The constitutional enshrinement of nuclear status in April 2012 proved consequential: it signaled that nuclear weapons were no longer a bargaining chip for potential diplomacy but a permanent feature of North Korean identity. Subsequent missile and nuclear tests — including the first successful hydrogen bomb test in January 2016 and the ICBM test in July 2017 — built directly on the organizational and ideological foundations laid in 2012.

## Key Facts

- Kim Jong-il died: December 17, 2011; Kim Jong-un publicly declared Supreme Commander of the KPA on December 24, 2011, and formally appointed December 30, 2011
- April 13, 2012: Unha-3 rocket launch failure — debris into Yellow Sea ~90 seconds after launch
- April 15, 2012: Kim Il-sung centennial parade; Kim Jong-un's first public speech
- July 2012: General Ri Yong-ho removed as Chief of the General Staff
- December 12, 2012: Successful Unha-3 / Kwangmyongsong-3 Unit 2 satellite launch
- Kim Jong-un's estimated age at succession: 28 (birth year disputed between 1983 and 1984)
