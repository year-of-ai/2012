# Vladimir Putin — 2012 Return to Presidency

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Vladimir Vladimirovich Putin (returning president), Dmitry Medvedev (outgoing president, prime minister), Alexei Navalny (opposition leader), Mikhail Prokhorov (independent presidential candidate)

## Summary

Vladimir Putin was inaugurated as president of Russia on May 7, 2012, beginning his third presidential term (his second under the title of the Russian Federation). His return marked the culmination of a "tandem" power arrangement orchestrated since 2008: a constitutional amendment passed that year extended the presidential term from four to six years, and Putin had served as prime minister under Dmitry Medvedev to preserve formal compliance with constitutional limits on consecutive terms. Putin's victory in the March 4, 2012 presidential election — with 63.6% of the vote in the first round — came despite mass protests in Moscow triggered by the disputed December 2011 parliamentary elections. His return to the Kremlin signaled not only a consolidation of political power but also an inflection point in Russia's domestic governance model and its posture toward the West.

## The 2012 Presidential Election

### March 4 Vote

The March 4, 2012 presidential election produced the following first-round results:
- **Vladimir Putin**: 63.6% (45.6 million votes)
- **Gennady Zyuganov** (Communist Party): 17.2%
- **Mikhail Prokhorov** (independent): 7.98%
- **Vladimir Zhirinovsky** (LDPR): 6.2%
- **Sergei Mironov** (Just Russia): 3.85%

International and domestic observers noted procedural violations, ballot-stuffing allegations, and restrictions on opposition candidate registration, but no Western government declined to recognize the result. The Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe (OSCE) election observation mission cited "serious problems" in its preliminary report, though it stopped short of declaring the election fraudulent.

### Constitutional and Structural Context

The amendment extending the presidential term from four to six years, passed during Medvedev's presidency in 2008, meant that Putin's 2012 victory would give him a term extending through 2018. A subsequent amendment in 2020 further reset Putin's term count, allowing him to remain eligible through 2036. The 2012 inauguration thus began what critics described as indefinite single-person rule under democratic formalism.

## The Protest Movement

### Bolotnaya Square and the "For Fair Elections" Movement

Putin's return to the presidency was accompanied by the most significant anti-Kremlin protests in Moscow since the Soviet era. The protests were triggered by the December 4, 2011 State Duma elections, in which United Russia officially received 49.3% — a sharp drop from 64.3% in 2007 — amid widespread allegations of fraud documented in social media video evidence. Tens of thousands gathered at Chistye Prudy on December 5, 2011 and at Bolotnaya Square on December 10, 2011, with estimated attendance of 50,000–80,000.

On May 6, 2012 — the day before Putin's inauguration — violence erupted at Bolotnaya Square when police attempted to disperse a permitted protest. Approximately 100,000 marchers assembled; between 400 and 600 protesters were arrested in clashes with riot police. The event became known as the "Bolotnaya Case," and subsequent prosecutions of protest participants drew international condemnation. Aleksei Navalny, a prominent opposition blogger and anti-corruption activist, was among those arrested. The Bolotnaya prosecutions, which resulted in prison sentences of up to 4.5 years for protest organizers, were widely seen as exemplifying the tightening of political repression following Putin's return.

## Domestic Policy Consolidation

After his inauguration, Putin moved quickly to curtail civil society organizations and restrict political opposition. The 2012 "foreign agents" law (July 2012) required non-governmental organizations receiving foreign funding and engaged in broadly defined "political activity" to register as foreign agents — a Soviet-era designation carrying strong stigma. Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch characterized the law as designed to stigmatize independent civil society. In the same period, penalties for unauthorized protests were sharply increased, with fines raised from 5,000 rubles to 300,000 rubles.

## Geopolitical Implications

### Syria Vetoes

Putin's return to the presidency was immediately felt in international diplomacy. Russia vetoed UN Security Council resolutions on Syria on February 4, 2012 (before his inauguration, reflecting continuity with the tandem-era foreign policy) and again on July 19, 2012. These vetoes blocked international accountability mechanisms and arms embargoes against the Assad government during the intensifying Syrian civil war, aligning Russia with Iran against the Western-Arab bloc. The Russian and Chinese vetoes drew sharp criticism from the United States and European Union but reflected Putin's strategic calculation that Western-backed regime change in Syria would set an unacceptable precedent.

### NATO and the "Reset" Collapse

U.S.-Russia relations had been conducted under the "Reset" framework initiated by Secretary of State Hillary Clinton and Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov in 2009. Putin's return coincided with a deterioration of this relationship. Putin declined to attend the G8 summit in May 2012 (sending Medvedev as his substitute) and was openly critical of U.S. democracy promotion activities inside Russia. His September 2012 decision to expel USAID from Russia — claiming it was funding political opposition — was a symbolic rupture that reflected his suspicion of Western civil society engagement.
