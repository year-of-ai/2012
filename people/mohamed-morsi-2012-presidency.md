# Mohamed Morsi — 2012 Egyptian Presidency and Muslim Brotherhood

**Category**: People

**Key figures**: Mohamed Morsi (Muslim Brotherhood leader, Egyptian President), Ahmad Shafiq (Morsi's run-off opponent), Field Marshal Mohamed Hussein Tantawi (SCAF chairman), Hosni Mubarak (ousted president), Abdel Fattah el-Sisi (Defence Minister who later overthrew Morsi)

## Summary

Mohamed Morsi (Arabic: محمد مرسي; born August 20, 1951, Al Sharqiyya governorate, Egypt) was elected Egypt's first civilian and first Islamist president on June 30, 2012, following the 2011 Arab Spring uprising that removed Hosni Mubarak after 30 years in power. Running as the candidate of the Freedom and Justice Party — the electoral wing of the Muslim Brotherhood — Morsi won the presidential run-off with 13,230,131 votes (51.73%) against former prime minister Ahmad Shafiq's 12,347,380 (48.27%), on a turnout of approximately 51.8% of registered voters.

His presidency lasted exactly one year and four days before he was ousted in a military coup led by Defence Minister General Abdel Fattah el-Sisi on July 3, 2013. The year of Morsi's presidency — June 2012 to July 2013 — was defined by his rapid attempt to consolidate Islamist control over Egypt's state institutions, a move that provoked mass protests and ultimately provided the military with the pretext it sought to intervene. Morsi died in a Cairo courtroom on June 17, 2019, collapsing during a hearing while held in solitary confinement, after six years in detention.

## Background: Mohamed Morsi and the Muslim Brotherhood

Morsi was educated as an engineer, receiving a PhD from the University of Southern California in 1982, where he worked briefly as an assistant professor before returning to Egypt. He joined the Muslim Brotherhood, founded in 1928 by Hassan al-Banna, which had survived decades of state repression — including mass arrests under both Gamal Abdel Nasser and Mubarak — to become Egypt's most organized political force.

During Mubarak's final decade, Morsi served in the People's Assembly (2000–2005), running — like all the Brotherhood's candidates — officially as an independent because the organization was barred from fielding candidates under its own banner. He later became a senior member of the Brotherhood's Guidance Bureau, the body that directs the organization's strategy. He was imprisoned by Mubarak twice — once in 2006 and again in January 2011, during the early days of the Tahrir Square uprising, escaping or being released amid the security breakdown.

The Brotherhood's Freedom and Justice Party (FJP) was founded in April 2011, weeks after Mubarak fell. In the parliamentary elections of November 2011–January 2012, the FJP won 235 of 498 parliamentary seats (47.2%), making it the dominant force in Egypt's post-revolutionary legislature.

## The 2012 Presidential Election

Egypt's presidential election was conducted in two rounds in May and June 2012 under the oversight of the Supreme Military Council (SCAF), which had governed the country since Mubarak's resignation on February 11, 2011.

**First round (May 23–24, 2012)**:

| Candidate | Affiliation | Votes | Percentage |
|-----------|-------------|-------|------------|
| Mohamed Morsi | Freedom and Justice Party | 5,764,952 | 24.78% |
| Ahmad Shafiq | Independent (former military/PM) | 5,505,327 | 23.66% |
| Hamdeen Sabahi | Nasserist | 4,820,273 | 20.72% |
| Abdel Moneim Aboul Fotouh | Independent Islamist | 4,065,239 | 17.47% |
| Amr Moussa | Former Arab League Secretary-General | 2,588,850 | 11.13% |

The narrow first-round result alarmed many Egyptian liberals and leftists, who had backed Hamdeen Sabahi: the run-off choice between the Brotherhood's candidate and a Mubarak-era general forced many to hold their nose and vote for Morsi as the lesser evil.

**Run-off (June 16–17, 2012)**:

The Presidential Electoral Commission announced the result on June 24 after a tense delay — during which SCAF dissolved the elected parliament using a Supreme Constitutional Court ruling — declaring Morsi the winner with 51.73% of the vote. He was inaugurated at Cairo University on June 30, 2012, with a second ceremonial oath taken at Tahrir Square in an act of political symbolism.

Critically, in the days between the run-off vote and the announcement of results, SCAF issued a constitutional addendum on June 17, 2012, stripping the incoming president of military oversight, budgetary authority, and the right to declare war — constraints that significantly limited Morsi's formal power before he had even taken office.

## The November 2012 Constitutional Declaration

The gravest domestic crisis of Morsi's presidency came on November 22, 2012, when he issued a sweeping constitutional declaration that:

- **Immunized his own decisions** from judicial review, declaring them final and binding on all state bodies pending the completion of a new constitution.
- **Dismissed the Hosni Mubarak-era prosecutor-general**, Abdel Meguid Mahmoud, and appointed Talaat Ibrahim in his place.
- **Ordered retrials** of those acquitted in Mubarak-era corruption cases.
- **Shielded the Constituent Assembly** — the 100-member body tasked with drafting a new constitution, which was dominated by Islamists — from dissolution by the courts.

The decree triggered what opponents called a "constitutional coup." Large protests broke out across Egypt, with tens of thousands gathering at Tahrir Square in the most significant demonstrations since 2011. The National Salvation Front, a coalition of liberals and leftists, was formed in direct response, bringing together Mohammed ElBaradei, Amr Moussa, and former presidential candidate Hamdeen Sabahi.

Under intense pressure, Morsi rescinded most of the decree on December 8, 2012, but the constitutional assembly moved swiftly: it approved a draft constitution on December 1, which was then put to a referendum on December 15–22, 2012. The constitution passed with **63.8%** in favour, but on a turnout of only approximately **32.9%** of eligible voters — widely cited as indicating the absence of genuine societal consensus.

## Regional and Foreign Policy

Morsi navigated a volatile regional environment during his year in office:

- **Gaza ceasefire (November 2012)**: Morsi brokered a ceasefire between Israel and Hamas — Hamas being an offshoot of the Muslim Brotherhood — following an eight-day escalation in November. US Secretary of State Hillary Clinton visited Cairo for the negotiations, and the Obama administration credited Morsi with effective mediation. This represented one of Morsi's few internationally praised diplomatic moments.
- **Syrian Civil War**: Morsi broke with the Muslim Brotherhood's earlier caution and became one of the more vocal Arab leaders calling for Bashar al-Assad to step down. At an August 2012 Non-Aligned Movement summit in Tehran, he directly condemned the Syrian government in the presence of Iran's leadership — a pointed act given that Iran was Assad's principal foreign backer.
- **Relations with the United States**: The Obama administration was cautious and transactional in its dealings with Morsi. US aid to Egypt — approximately $1.5 billion annually, of which $1.3 billion was military assistance — continued throughout, though congressional pressure to condition aid on human rights criteria grew.
- **Ethiopian Nile dam dispute**: Morsi was caught in June 2013 on open microphone during a broadcast of an unrelated meeting; Egyptian political figures were shown discussing covert options including "making life difficult" for Ethiopia over its Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam project — a serious diplomatic embarrassment.

## Ouster and Aftermath

On June 30, 2013 — the first anniversary of Morsi's inauguration — mass protests organized by the Tamarod ("Rebel") movement brought millions of Egyptians to the streets. On July 3, Defence Minister General Abdel Fattah el-Sisi announced that Morsi had been removed, a new constitutional declaration issued, and interim President Adly Mahmoud Mansour installed. Egyptian state television was taken off the air; Brotherhood leaders, including Morsi, were arrested.

The Brotherhood characterized the events as a coup; the military and its supporters described them as answering a popular mandate. The United States declined to formally label the events a coup, a classification that would have legally required suspension of military aid.

Morsi was tried on multiple charges simultaneously: incitement to murder protesters (December 2014, sentenced to 20 years); espionage with Qatar (September 2016, sentenced to 40 years); spying for Hamas and Hezbollah (May 2015, sentenced to life); and insulting the judiciary (December 2017, sentenced to 3 years). He was held in solitary confinement at Wadi el-Natrun prison in conditions his family and international rights organizations described as deliberately punitive.

On June 17, 2019, Morsi collapsed inside the glass-and-metal cage in which defendants are held during Egyptian court proceedings, and died before medical assistance could reach him. He was 67. The UN Special Rapporteur on extrajudicial executions stated that the conditions of his detention had "placed his life at serious risk" and called for a UK government inquiry (given UK monitoring of his conditions). He was buried in a pre-dawn funeral in Cairo attended only by his family.

## Significance

Morsi's 2012 presidency is a pivotal case study in the failures of post-revolutionary democratic transitions:

- **The Brotherhood's electoral success** demonstrated that decades of suppression had not eliminated Islamist politics; it had merely concentrated it within the best-organized civil society network Egypt possessed.
- **The speed of his overreach** — from June 30, 2012 to the November constitutional declaration is less than five months — reflected a fundamental miscalculation of how much formal electoral legitimacy could substitute for broader coalition-building.
- **The military's structural dominance** was never genuinely disturbed. SCAF's June 2012 constitutional addendum ensured that the military's institutional autonomy, budget, and impunity were preserved regardless of who held the presidency.
- **Regional resonance**: Egypt's trajectory was watched across the region — in Libya, Tunisia, Yemen, and Syria — by movements and governments asking whether the Arab Spring would produce durable democratic transitions or new authoritarian settlements. Morsi's fall, and the consolidation of el-Sisi's rule from 2014, was widely read as a decisive answer.

The Egyptian uprising of 2011 had been one of the defining events of the Arab Spring. Morsi's election, presidency, and ouster compressed what would become the decade's dominant regional narrative — democratic aspiration, Islamist electoral success, and military restoration — into a single year.
