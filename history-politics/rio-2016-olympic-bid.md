# Rio 2016 Olympic Bid Success

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (Brazilian president, 2009), Carlos Arthur Nuzman (Brazilian Olympic Committee president), Eduardo Paes (Rio de Janeiro mayor), Jacques Rogge (IOC president), Barack Obama (lobbied for Chicago bid)

## Overview

On **October 2, 2009**, at the **121st Session of the International Olympic Committee (IOC)** in Copenhagen, Denmark, Rio de Janeiro was selected as the host city for the **2016 Summer Olympics** — the first time any Olympic Games would be held in South America. Rio defeated three competing finalists — Madrid (Spain), Tokyo (Japan), and Chicago (USA) — in a three-round secret ballot conducted before the IOC General Assembly. The selection represented a landmark shift in Olympic geography toward the Global South, extending the Games' reach beyond their traditional wealthy-nation hosts.

## The Bid Decision

The IOC vote proceeded through three rounds, with the lowest-scoring city eliminated each round:

| Round | Chicago | Tokyo | Madrid | Rio |
|-------|---------|-------|--------|-----|
| 1st   | 18      | 22    | 28     | 26  |
| 2nd   | —       | 20    | 29     | 46  |
| Final | —       | —     | 32     | **66** |

Chicago's elimination in the first round — with only 18 votes — was widely considered the most stunning upset of any recent Olympic bid vote. U.S. President **Barack Obama** and First Lady **Michelle Obama** had flown to Copenhagen specifically to lobby the IOC on behalf of the Chicago bid, making Obama the first sitting U.S. president to personally advocate for an Olympic candidacy. The early elimination was reported as an embarrassment for the Obama White House.

In the final round, Rio won decisively with **66 votes** against Madrid's 32, far exceeding pre-vote projections. Brazilian President **Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva** had personally addressed the IOC session, delivering an emotional speech describing the Games as a recognition of Latin America's emergence. His presence — along with King Juan Carlos I of Spain advocating for Madrid, and Prime Minister Yukio Hatoyama for Tokyo — made the Copenhagen session an unusually high-level diplomatic gathering. Approximately **100,000 people** gathered on Copacabana Beach in Rio to watch the announcement live, and celebrations erupted across Brazil's major cities.

## Rio's Bid Strategy

Rio's candidacy, its fourth Olympic bid attempt (previous bids: 1936, 2004, 2012), rested on several interconnected arguments:

- **Geographic equity**: The bid emphasized that South America was the only inhabited continent never to have hosted the Summer Olympics; Rio argued this omission was overdue for correction
- **Urban transformation**: The proposal committed to extensive infrastructure investment — expansion of Rio's metro network, construction of a new bus rapid transit (BRT) corridor, environmental remediation of Guanabara Bay (an Olympic sailing venue), and renovation of the Maracanã stadium
- **Brazil's rising status**: The Lula government's economic record — GDP growth averaging approximately **4% annually** from 2003–2010, and more than **20 million Brazilians** lifted above the poverty line during the same period — provided credibility for Brazil's capacity to host
- **Global recognition**: The Christ the Redeemer (Cristo Redentor) statue featured prominently in bid imagery, leveraging one of the most globally recognizable landmarks to anchor the Olympic identity

The Rio bid committee spent approximately **$48.8 million** preparing its candidacy over several years.

## Historical Significance

Rio's selection made it the first South American Olympic host city. Prior Southern Hemisphere Summer Olympic hosts had been limited to Melbourne (1956) and Sydney (2000), both in Australia. The decision extended the Games' geographic footprint for the first time since Sydney and came during a period widely seen as a high-water mark of developing-world confidence — Brazil was simultaneously a BRICS nation with newly granted investment-grade credit ratings (from Standard & Poor's in April 2008, with Fitch following in May 2008) and the host of the 2014 FIFA World Cup.

By 2012 — three years after the selection — Rio was in active preparation, with Olympic construction projects underway across the Barra da Tijuca district, where the main Olympic Park would be located. The 2012 London Olympics included the formal handover ceremony that began the four-year countdown to Rio 2016, reinforcing Brazil's place in the Olympic succession.

## Legacy and Complications

The 2016 Games ultimately took place during a severe Brazilian political and economic crisis. Brazil entered recession in 2015; President Dilma Rousseff was impeached in August 2016 as the Olympics began; and the Games were shadowed by concerns about Zika virus, heavily polluted Olympic water venues, and elevated security costs. Total infrastructure costs exceeded **$12 billion**, far above original projections. Several Olympic venues fell into disrepair after the Games, drawing comparisons to post-Olympic "white elephant" sites in other host cities.

A particularly damaging postscript involved the bid itself: in 2017, Carlos Arthur Nuzman — who had led Brazil's Olympic campaign — was arrested on charges connected to bribing IOC members to secure Rio's selection. He was convicted in November 2021 and sentenced to 30 years and 11 months in prison on charges including corruption, criminal organization, and money laundering, though he remained free pending appeals and continued to contest the verdict.
