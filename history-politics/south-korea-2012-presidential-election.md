# South Korea 2012 Presidential Election

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Park Geun-hye (Saenuri Party, president-elect), Moon Jae-in (Democratic United Party), Ahn Cheol-soo (independent, withdrew), Lee Myung-bak (outgoing president)

## Overview

On **December 19, 2012**, South Korea held its **18th presidential election**, electing **Park Geun-hye** of the ruling Saenuri (New Frontier) Party as the nation's 18th president with **51.55% of the vote** (15,773,128 votes). She defeated Moon Jae-in of the Democratic United Party, who received **48.02%** (14,692,632 votes). Voter turnout reached **75.8%** — the highest since 1997. The margin of approximately **1.08 million votes** was the narrowest presidential victory since South Korea's 1987 democratic transition. Park's election made her the **first female president in South Korean history** and the first woman directly elected as head of state in East Asia.

## Background and Candidates

South Korea's constitution limits presidents to a single five-year term, barring re-election. Outgoing President **Lee Myung-bak** (served 2008–2013), also of the Saenuri Party, was constitutionally ineligible to run.

### Park Geun-hye

Park (born February 2, 1952) was a veteran four-term National Assembly member and the daughter of former President **Park Chung-hee**, who governed South Korea from 1963 until his assassination in October 1979. Her father's legacy was deeply contested: simultaneously remembered as the architect of South Korea's export-led economic miracle — per capita GDP rose from approximately $100 in 1963 to $1,700 by 1979 — and as an authoritarian ruler who suppressed civil liberties, dissolved the National Assembly, and ruled by decree under the Yushin Constitution.

In **August 1974**, a North Korean sympathizer named Mun Se-gwang attempted to assassinate Park Chung-hee during Liberation Day ceremonies in Seoul; the attacker missed the president but shot and killed his wife, **Yuk Young-soo** — Park Geun-hye's mother. The 22-year-old Park subsequently assumed a quasi-first-lady role for the remaining years of her father's administration.

### Moon Jae-in

Moon (born January 24, 1953) was a human rights lawyer and close ally of progressive President **Roh Moo-hyun** (2003–2008), having served as Roh's presidential chief of staff. Roh's 2009 suicide — following a corruption investigation shortly after leaving office — was a defining emotional touchstone for the liberal opposition and shaped Moon's entire political identity.

### Ahn Cheol-soo

Software entrepreneur **Ahn Cheol-soo** initially polled in the high 20s and represented a centrist alternative to both major parties. He withdrew from the race in **November 2012** and endorsed Moon, hoping to consolidate the anti-Saenuri vote — but the late withdrawal disrupted the opposition campaign's momentum in its final weeks.

## Campaign Issues

The election turned on several interconnected issues:

- ***Chaebol* reform and economic democratization**: South Korea's economy was dominated by large family-controlled conglomerates (Samsung, Hyundai, LG, SK Group, Lotte). Park surprised observers by adopting "economic democratization" language — a progressive framing that acknowledged public unease about corporate concentration — while Moon proposed more direct regulatory intervention
- **North Korea policy**: Park advocated a cautious "Trustpolitik" or trust-building process with Pyongyang, replacing the liberal "Sunshine Policy" engagement of the Roh era; Moon favored renewed economic engagement and dialogue
- **Social welfare expansion**: Moon proposed significantly expanded healthcare coverage, education support, and retirement benefits; Park emphasized job creation through growth
- **NIS election interference**: Shortly before election day, allegations emerged that the National Intelligence Service (NIS) had conducted psychological operations on domestic social media — posting anti-Moon comments through dozens of operatives — in violation of election law. NIS director **Won Sei-hoon** was later convicted in 2014 for directing these operations, raising lasting questions about the legitimacy of the 2012 result

## Election Results

| Candidate | Party | Votes | Percentage |
|-----------|-------|-------|------------|
| Park Geun-hye | Saenuri Party | 15,773,128 | 51.55% |
| Moon Jae-in | Democratic United Party | 14,692,632 | 48.02% |
| Others | Various | ~255,000 | 0.43% |

**Registered voters**: 40,507,842  
**Turnout**: 30,721,459 (75.8%)

Park performed strongest in the conservative **Gyeongsang provinces** of the southeast (her father's base) and among voters aged 50 and above. Moon dominated in **Jeolla province** (the traditional progressive stronghold) and among voters under 40. Turnout was exceptionally high in both bases, reflecting the intensity of partisan mobilization.

## Park's Presidency and Removal from Office

Park Geun-hye was inaugurated on **February 25, 2013**. Her presidency launched the "Northeast Asia Peace and Cooperation Initiative" as a regional diplomatic framework. Within weeks, it faced its first major test: North Korea conducted its **third nuclear test** on **February 12, 2013**, generating widespread international condemnation and heightening inter-Korean tension.

Her administration's later years were dominated by a corruption scandal: revelations emerged in October 2016 that Park had allowed her close confidante **Choi Soon-sil** — a private citizen with no government position — to access classified state documents, influence cabinet appointments, and extract approximately **70 billion won** (around $60 million) in "donations" from major *chaebol* to foundations Choi controlled.

Mass candlelight protests — the **Candlelight Revolution** — brought millions of demonstrators to central Seoul over consecutive weekends from October to December 2016. The National Assembly voted to **impeach** Park on **December 9, 2016**, with 234 of 300 legislators voting in favor. The Constitutional Court unanimously upheld the impeachment on **March 10, 2017**, permanently removing her from office.

Park was convicted of bribery, abuse of power, and coercion, and ultimately sentenced to **22 years** in prison. President **Moon Jae-in** — her 2012 opponent — pardoned her on **December 24, 2021**, citing humanitarian grounds related to her health.

## Significance

The 2012 election demonstrated both the vitality and the fragility of South Korean democracy. A 75.8% turnout in a competitive, nationally televised race showed a politically engaged electorate; the subsequent NIS scandal, Park's impeachment, and conviction showed the risks of concentrated executive power and compromised institutional independence.

Park's election as the first female president was simultaneously a historic milestone and a reminder that South Korean women remained dramatically underrepresented in politics: the National Assembly was less than 16% female at the time, and Park's ascent owed significantly to her political dynasty rather than to broader gender parity in the political system.

The closeness of the result — 51.55% versus 48.02% — reflected a deeply polarized electorate divided along generational, regional, and class lines, a division that would persist through subsequent electoral cycles.
