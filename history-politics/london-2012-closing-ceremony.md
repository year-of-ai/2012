# 2012 Summer Olympics Closing Ceremony

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Kim Gavin (creative director/choreographer), Es Devlin (set designer), David Arnold (musical director), Stephen Daldry (executive producer), Damien Hirst (Union Flag installation), Eric Idle (performer), The Who, Spice Girls, George Michael, Ed Sheeran, Muse

## Summary

On **August 12, 2012**, at **21:00 BST**, the London Summer Olympics concluded with a three-hour-and-eleven-minute closing ceremony held at the 80,000-seat **Olympic Stadium** in Stratford, East London. Titled informally as **"A Symphony of British Music"**, the event was directed and choreographed by **Kim Gavin**, with set design by **Es Devlin**, musical direction by **David Arnold**, and executive production by **Stephen Daldry**. The ceremony featured approximately **4,100 performers**, including 3,500 adult volunteers and 380 schoolchildren, performing under a total budget of approximately **£20 million**; performing artists were nominally paid **£1 each** under a symbolic contractual arrangement.

The spectacle traced five decades of British popular music — from the 1960s through to 2012 — and concluded with the formal handover of the Olympic flag to the mayor of Rio de Janeiro, marking the start of the four-year countdown to the [2016 Summer Olympics](rio-2016-olympic-bid.md).

## Production and Design

Kim Gavin, best known as the creative director for Take That's arena tours, was selected to lead the ceremony after Danny Boyle — who had directed the acclaimed [opening ceremony](london-2012-opening-ceremony.md) — chose not to continue for the closing event. The two ceremonies were deliberately designed in contrast: where Boyle's "Isles of Wonder" narrated British history and social values with theatrical complexity, Gavin's vision was explicitly celebratory, a party rather than a statement.

**Es Devlin's** set transformed the stadium floor into a **Union Flag** design conceived by visual artist **Damien Hirst** — filling the entire arena with the red, white, and blue of the British national flag. The design referenced Hirst's distinctive spot-painting style and his broader association with the British cultural establishment. The production incorporated **40 double-decker buses**, a fleet of black London taxis, and elaborate lighting rigs integrating thousands of individual LED units.

Rehearsals were conducted over several weeks at the stadium and at an external facility, with performers and crew under the same strict non-disclosure agreements used for the opening ceremony.

## Sequence of Segments

### Parade of Nations
Athletes from all **206 competing National Olympic Committees** entered the stadium in a single combined procession rather than the traditional nation-by-nation parade, wearing their national team uniforms but mingling freely — a deliberate symbolic choice emphasizing Olympic community over national competition. Great Britain's team, having won **65 medals (29 gold)** across the Games, entered to particular acclaim from the 80,000-strong crowd.

### "Symphony of British Music"
The centrepiece of the ceremony was a sweeping musical retrospective covering five decades:

- **Russell Brand** appeared as Willy Wonka astride a giant locomotive, performing a verse of "Pure Imagination" before introducing the musical sections
- **Madness** performed "Our House" from the stadium roof while the floor below them was transformed into a street scene, projecting a version of the Madness music video across the arena
- **Pet Shop Boys** performed "West End Girls" and "It's a Sin"
- **George Michael** performed "Freedom '90" and the new single "White Light" — his first major public performance in several years following a serious illness in 2011 that had nearly killed him (a severe case of pneumonia in Vienna that November had left him hospitalised for three weeks)
- **Annie Lennox** performed "Little Bird" aboard a tall ship, a performance widely praised by critics
- **One Direction** performed "What Makes You Beautiful," reflecting the group's 2012 status as Britain's most commercially successful contemporary act
- **Ed Sheeran** performed Pink Floyd's "Wish You Were Here," backed by Pink Floyd drummer Nick Mason, Genesis' Mike Rutherford, and The Feeling's Richard Jones, while a tightrope walker crossed high above the arena
- **Muse** performed "Survival" — the official anthem of the London 2012 Olympics — as well as "Uprising"
- **Emeli Sandé** performed "Read All About It (Pt. III)," having also opened the [opening ceremony](london-2012-opening-ceremony.md) with "Abide with Me"; her double appearance was widely noted as a mark of her emergence as a major British artist in 2012
- **Take That** performed "Rule the World" and "Never Forget"
- **The Spice Girls** reunited for the first time since the closing ceremony of the **2007 world reunion tour**, performing "Wannabe" and "Spice Up Your Life" while arriving on the roofs of five black London taxis driving in formation around the stadium
- **The Who** — represented by surviving members **Roger Daltrey** and **Pete Townshend** — closed the musical section with a four-song set including "My Generation," "Pinball Wizard," "Baba O'Riley," and "See Me, Feel Me / Listening to You"

A tribute to **Freddie Mercury** was delivered through an archival recording of his theatrical audience warm-up from a 1986 Wembley Stadium concert, projected across screens while the crowd responded in call-and-response — recreating the original Wembley exchange with 80,000 people in real time. The surviving members of **Queen**, guitarist **Brian May** and drummer **Roger Taylor**, then took the stage — May opening with part of the "Brighton Rock" guitar solo — before singer **Jessie J** joined them for a performance of "We Will Rock You."

### Eric Idle and Monty Python
**Eric Idle** performed **"Always Look on the Bright Side of Life"** — the satirical anthem from *Monty Python's Life of Brian* (1979) — in a sequence that became one of the ceremony's most memorable moments. Idle led the assembled crowd, athletes, and performers in a mass singalong, with volunteers in silver suits performing a choreographed dance routine. The choice of a song about cheerfully accepting suffering as a closer to the main entertainment block was widely interpreted as characteristic British self-deprecation and irony.

### Olympic Flag Handover
The Olympic flag was formally received by the Mayor of Rio de Janeiro, **Eduardo Paes**, from London Mayor **Boris Johnson**. Brazilian football legend **Pelé** — 71 years old at the time — appeared in the handover segment, titled **"Embrace,"** which featured Brazilian dancers, percussionists, and a performance of Brazilian popular music. The segment offered a preview of Rio's cultural identity in advance of the 2016 Games.

Pelé's appearance was significant: he had never attended an Olympic closing ceremony as a representative of an incoming host nation, and his presence at the event cemented the Rio 2016 handover as a geopolitically meaningful broadcast moment — a statement about Brazil's global status at the peak of its pre-crisis international confidence (see [Rio 2016 Olympic Bid Success](rio-2016-olympic-bid.md)).

### Extinguishing of the Olympic Flame
The Olympic cauldron — Thomas Heatherwick's assemblage of **204 copper petals**, one for each competing nation, which had been lit during the opening ceremony and burned throughout the Games — was extinguished as the final act. The ceremony closed with a fireworks display at **00:07 BST on August 13**.

## Viewership and Reception

The ceremony was broadcast in the United Kingdom on **BBC One**, reaching an average audience of **23.2 million** with peak audiences above 26 million — making it the second-most-watched UK television event of 2012 after the opening ceremony. Worldwide, the event reached an estimated **750 million viewers**, consistent with the global reach of the Games' other major ceremonies.

Critical reception was broadly positive. Reviewers praised the Spice Girls reunion, Eric Idle's performance, and Annie Lennox's haunting ship performance as highlights. The Who received sustained applause for their closing set despite mixed reviews about the amplification quality inside the stadium. Some critics found the ceremony less artistically cohesive than Boyle's opening, noting that Gavin's "greatest hits" structure prioritised entertainment over narrative.

The BBC broadcast, hosted by Hazel Irvine, Huw Edwards, and Sue Barker, opened with a summary of Team GB's medal performances across the Games.

## Significance

### Celebration of British Pop Identity
The ceremony functioned as a deliberate and self-confident statement of British soft power through popular culture — an argument, made through music, that the United Kingdom had contributed disproportionately to global popular music relative to its size. The sequence spanning Madness to The Who provided a compressed history of British pop from ska revival to stadium rock to girl groups to indie, encompassing genres that dominated global charts across five decades. The same cultural confidence had been visible in the opening ceremony's "Symphony of British Music" segment and in the global reach of acts like [Adele](../arts-culture/adeles-21-album.md), whose *21* album had dominated world charts through 2012.

### Template for Post-Olympic Celebrations
The 2012 closing ceremony established an influential template: less nationalistic triumphalism than opening counterparts; more inclusive, cosmopolitan celebration of contemporary culture; and a deliberate platform for the incoming host city's introduction to a global audience. Rio 2016 and subsequent ceremonies adopted similar structures — with the handover segment to the next host becoming an established ceremony-within-a-ceremony requiring its own creative direction.

### Rio Handover as Geopolitical Broadcast
The "Embrace" segment's formal handover to Rio represented a historically significant moment: the first time a South American city was receiving the Olympic flag in a closing ceremony. For an estimated 750 million viewers, the segment served as the global introduction of Brazil as Olympic host — the beginning of a four-year narrative arc that would culminate with the 2016 Games. The visible presence of Pelé and Brazilian cultural performers was a deliberate bid to shape global perceptions of Brazil at a moment of significant international prestige, three years before the political and economic crises that would shadow the actual 2016 Games.
