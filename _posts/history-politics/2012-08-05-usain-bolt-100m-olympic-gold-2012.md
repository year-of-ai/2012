## Summary

On August 5, 2012, Jamaican sprinter Usain Bolt won the men's 100-meter final at the London Summer Olympics with a time of 9.63 seconds, setting a new Olympic record. Bolt's victory represented his successful defense of the Olympic title he had won in Beijing in 2008, making him only the second man in Olympic history to accomplish the feat (the first being American Carl Lewis in 1984 and 1988). The race itself was historically significant for its unprecedented depth: seven of the eight finalists broke 10 seconds—the first time that had happened in an Olympic 100-metre final—and the top three finishers all broke 9.80 seconds, another Olympic first. Only Asafa Powell, hampered by a groin injury, fell off the pace.

Running in lane 7 at the Olympic Stadium before approximately 80,000 spectators, Bolt accelerated from the blocks and dominated the final 60 meters. Yohan Blake of Jamaica claimed silver with 9.75 seconds, and American Justin Gatlin took bronze with 9.79 seconds. Wind conditions were measured at +1.5 m/s, within the legal limit for record eligibility. Bolt's 9.63 seconds stands as an Olympic record; his world record of 9.58 seconds, set at the 2009 World Championships in Berlin, remains unbroken as of 2026.

## Road to London: The 2012 Season

Bolt arrived in London with less certainty surrounding his form than had preceded his dominant 2008 campaign. In June 2012, he lost twice to training partner and Jamaican rival Yohan Blake—first in the 100 metres at the Jamaican Olympic Trials on June 29 (running 9.86 to Blake's 9.75), then in the 200 metres final. Blake's rise prompted widespread speculation that the changing of the guard had arrived. Bolt had also battled a recurring Achilles tendon injury and was working with physiotherapists to maintain peak condition.

Coach Glen Mills, who had guided Bolt since 2004, made a series of technical adjustments to Bolt's start—historically his weakest phase—in the months prior to the Games. These refinements proved decisive: Bolt's reaction time at the London final was 0.165 seconds, respectable for an athlete whose height of 6 feet 5 inches (1.95 m) poses leverage disadvantages at the blocks.

## The Race: Analysis and Splits

The men's 100-metre final was held at 21:50 local time on August 5. Bolt's performance broke down across three mechanical phases:

- **0–30 m (drive phase)**: Bolt ranked fourth among finalists, demonstrating that his start remained a relative weakness. His large stride frequency disadvantage versus shorter competitors was evident in the opening strides.
- **30–60 m (transition phase)**: Bolt's top-end speed mechanics began to assert dominance, as his stride length—estimated at approximately 2.44 m (8 ft) at maximum velocity—compensated for lower cadence.
- **60–100 m (maximum velocity/maintenance)**: Bolt reached an estimated peak speed of 12.4 m/s (44.6 km/h or 27.8 mph) and maintained near-maximum velocity through the tape, a phase in which no competitor could match him.

Yohan Blake's 9.75 silver-medal time set a national record for Jamaica's second-best sprinter. Justin Gatlin's bronze at 9.79 seconds was remarkable given that Gatlin had served a doping ban from 2006 to 2010—his performance in London reignited ongoing debate about second chances and sports integrity (see also: [Lance Armstrong USADA Investigation]({{ '/news/society-economics/lance-armstrong-usada-investigation-2012/' | relative_url }})).

## London 2012 Context

The 100-metre final was one of the marquee events of the [2012 London Olympics]({{ '/news/history-politics/london-2012-olympics/' | relative_url }}), which ran from July 27 to August 12. The Games were themselves staged at a moment of intense national pride in the United Kingdom, coming less than two months after the [Queen Elizabeth Diamond Jubilee]({{ '/news/history-politics/queen-elizabeth-diamond-jubilee-2012/' | relative_url }}). Organizers had deliberately scheduled the 100-metre final for August 5—nicknamed "Super Saturday" in British media—alongside British athletic success in the heptathlon (Jessica Ennis-Hill) and long jump (Greg Rutherford), making it the most decorated single evening for the host nation in a century of Olympic competition.

Bolt's celebration—his customary lightning-bolt pose and exuberant engagement with the crowd—produced some of the most widely reproduced images of the Games, staged in the same Olympic Stadium that had hosted Danny Boyle's [opening ceremony]({{ '/news/history-politics/london-2012-opening-ceremony/' | relative_url }}) nine days earlier. The men's 100-metre final was among the most-watched events of London 2012, drawing a worldwide television audience estimated in the hundreds of millions.

## World Record Context

Bolt's Olympic record of 9.63 seconds is 0.05 seconds slower than his own world record of 9.58 seconds, set at the Berlin World Championships on August 16, 2009. Biomechanical analyses of the Berlin run put his peak speed at roughly 12.3–12.4 m/s (about 44 km/h). The London final was run with a legal tailwind of +1.5 m/s—within the +2.0 m/s ceiling permitted for record purposes—and stands as a fully ratified Olympic record under World Athletics rules.

Bolt's sprinting has been the subject of published physical modelling. Analyses in physics journals—including a 2013 study in the *European Journal of Physics*—have modelled how aerodynamic drag shaped his Berlin performance, concluding that under more favourable conditions, or without his habitual deceleration before the line, he could have run appreciably faster. Researchers commonly attribute his dominance to unusually long strides—his stride length at top speed is estimated near 2.4 metres—which more than offset the slower limb turnover and slower start inherent to his 1.95 m frame.

## Legacy and Subsequent Career

Bolt went on to complete the "triple-triple" at London—winning gold in the 100 m, 200 m (time: 19.32 seconds), and 4×100 m relay (world record: 36.84 seconds)—becoming only the second man, after Carl Lewis, to win the 100 m at consecutive Olympics. He successfully defended all three titles at the 2016 Rio Olympics, retiring after the 2017 World Championships in London with eight Olympic gold medals and eleven World Championship gold medals.

The 2012 100-metre result stands as a snapshot of track and field's golden era for sprinting depth. The sub-9.80 sweep of the top three positions remains unmatched in Olympic history as of 2026.
