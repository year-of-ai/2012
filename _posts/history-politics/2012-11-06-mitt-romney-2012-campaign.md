## Summary

Mitt Romney, the former Governor of Massachusetts (2003–2007) and co-founder of private equity firm Bain Capital, sought the U.S. presidency in 2012. He formally announced his candidacy on June 2, 2011, in Stratham, New Hampshire. After a competitive primary season against a large Republican field that included Newt Gingrich, Rick Santorum, Ron Paul, and Rick Perry, Romney secured the party's presumptive nomination on May 29, 2012, after winning the Texas primary and accumulating 1,144 delegates — the threshold for clinching the nomination. He was formally nominated at the Republican National Convention in Tampa, Florida, August 27–30, 2012.

Romney selected U.S. Representative Paul Ryan of Wisconsin as his running mate on August 11, 2012, choosing the House Budget Committee chairman and architect of the controversial "Ryan budget," which proposed major restructuring of Medicare and federal spending. Ryan was known as a fiscal hawk and was intended to energize the conservative base.

The general election was defined by several landmark moments:

- **The "47 percent" video** (September 17, 2012): A video recorded at a private Boca Raton, Florida fundraiser on May 17 was released by *Mother Jones* magazine. In it, Romney stated that 47 percent of Americans "pay no income tax" and "believe they are victims" entitled to government support, and that his job was "not to worry about those people." The remarks dominated two weeks of news coverage and were cited by post-election analysts as a pivotal moment that crystallized negative perceptions of Romney among working-class voters.

- **The three presidential debates**: Romney was widely judged to have won the first debate (October 3, Denver), prompting a temporary surge in polls and narrowing what had been a 3–4 point Obama lead. Obama was judged to have won the second (October 16, Hofstra) and third (October 22, Boca Raton) debates, with the final debate focused on foreign policy.

- **Hurricane Sandy** (October 29–30): The storm struck the northeastern United States five days before the election, halting campaigning and allowing President Obama to demonstrate executive leadership in coordination with state officials, including Republican Governor Chris Christie of New Jersey, whose public praise of Obama's response drew criticism from Romney allies. See [Hurricane Sandy (2012)]({{ '/news/society-economics/hurricane-sandy-2012/' | relative_url }}).

## The Primary Season

The Republican primary was unusually prolonged. Romney, who had run in 2008 and built substantial campaign infrastructure, began as the nominal frontrunner but repeatedly saw rivals surge ahead in polls:

- **Rick Perry** peaked at roughly 30% in August–September 2011 before faltering in debates.
- **Herman Cain** led in some October 2011 polls before withdrawing over sexual harassment allegations in December.
- **Newt Gingrich** won the South Carolina primary (January 21, 2012) by a large margin, briefly threatening Romney's momentum.
- **Rick Santorum** won Minnesota, Missouri, and Colorado on February 7, 2012, effectively rebooting competition.

Romney won the Florida primary on January 31 with 46% of the vote (vs. Gingrich's 32%), an outcome decisively shaped by a $15.3 million Romney advertising blitz — the most spent in a single state primary to that point. By March, Romney had won seven of ten Super Tuesday states and the race was effectively decided, though Santorum remained until April 10, 2012.

## Campaign Strategy and Organization

Romney's campaign, headquartered in Boston, raised approximately $1.17 billion across the primary and general election cycles (including Super PAC spending), compared to Obama's approximately $1.12 billion — making 2012 the most expensive presidential election in U.S. history to that date. The campaign spent heavily on television advertising ($543 million by the campaigns and affiliated super PACs combined in the final stretch) but was outperformed by Obama's data operation.

Obama's reelection campaign built "Narwhal," a unified data platform that integrated voter contact, fundraising, and field operations, and employed a team of roughly 50 data scientists. The Romney campaign's own digital initiative, called "ORCA" (for "killer whale"), was intended to coordinate Election Day poll-watching and voter contact in real time, but crashed under load on November 6 and failed to function for most of the day — a widely publicized operational failure.

## Election Results

On November 6, 2012, Barack Obama won reelection with **332 electoral votes** and **51.1% of the popular vote** (65.9 million votes). Romney received **206 electoral votes** and **47.2% of the popular vote** (60.9 million votes). Key battleground states including Ohio, Virginia, Florida, Colorado, Nevada, Iowa, Wisconsin, and New Hampshire all went to Obama.

Exit polls highlighted decisive demographic gaps: Obama won 93% of African-American voters, 71% of Hispanic voters, 60% of voters aged 18–29, and 55% of women. Romney won 59% of white voters — the largest white-voter margin since Ronald Reagan — but demographic shifts made this insufficient.

Had Romney prevailed, he would have been the first member of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints to be elected president.

## Significance

The 2012 campaign reinforced several structural shifts in American elections:

1. **Data-driven campaigns**: The effectiveness of Obama's analytics operation against the failure of Romney's ORCA system accelerated investment by both parties in data infrastructure for future cycles.
2. **Demographic change as electoral math**: The 2012 results prompted the Republican National Committee's post-election "autopsy" report (released March 2013), which concluded the party needed to broaden its appeal to Latino, Asian-American, African-American, and younger voters.
3. **Super PAC spending**: The *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) decision enabled unprecedented outside spending; the 2012 cycle saw approximately $1 billion in super PAC expenditures, with Romney-aligned Restore Our Future PAC raising $153 million.
4. **The viral moment**: The "47 percent" video established that private fundraiser remarks could become defining public campaign events in the age of smartphones and social media.
