**Key figures**: Juan Manuel Santos (Colombian president), Timoleón Jiménez "Timochenko" (FARC commander-in-chief), Humberto de la Calle (lead government negotiator), Cuba and Norway (guarantor nations), Álvaro Uribe Vélez (former president and peace-process opponent)

## Summary

Juan Manuel Santos launched formal peace negotiations with the FARC (Fuerzas Armadas Revolucionarias de Colombia — Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia) on November 19, 2012, in Havana, Cuba, marking the beginning of the most significant attempt to end Latin America's longest-running armed conflict. The FARC had waged guerrilla warfare against the Colombian state since 1964 — a conflict that had claimed more than 220,000 lives and displaced approximately 6 million people over nearly five decades. Santos announced the negotiations publicly at the United Nations General Assembly in September 2012 after months of secret preliminary talks that began in February 2012 with Cuban and Norwegian mediation. His decision to negotiate while concurrently maintaining military pressure on the FARC represented a significant strategic departure from the purely military approach pursued by his predecessor and mentor, Álvaro Uribe.

## Santos's Background and the Strategic Pivot

### From Defense Minister to Peacemaker

Juan Manuel Santos served as Colombia's Minister of National Defense from 2006 to 2009 under President Álvaro Uribe, during which time he oversaw some of the most effective military operations against the FARC in the conflict's history. These included Operation Jaque on July 2, 2008 — a deceptive rescue operation that freed 15 hostages, including former presidential candidate Íngrid Betancourt, without a single shot fired — and the March 2008 bombing of FARC's second-in-command Raúl Reyes in Ecuadorian territory, which triggered a diplomatic crisis with Ecuador and Venezuela. Santos was elected president in 2010 largely on Uribe's backing and an implicit promise of continued hardline security policy.

The 2010–2012 shift in Santos's position was therefore politically perilous. By 2012, the Colombian military had killed or captured multiple senior FARC leaders, fragmenting the organization's command structure, yet the conflict showed no signs of military termination. Santos concluded that military pressure alone could not produce a final settlement and that a negotiated solution was both necessary and, for the first time, achievable from a position of state strength.

## The 2012 Negotiation Framework

### Preliminary Talks

Secret preliminary negotiations were conducted in Cuba beginning in February 2012, involving small delegations and mediated by Cuba and Norway. These talks produced a "General Agreement" framework — a six-point agenda publicly released on August 27, 2012, before formal talks were announced — that structured the entire four-year negotiation process. The six agenda points were:

1. **Integral Rural Development** — land access, rural reform, food security
2. **Political Participation** — conditions for former combatants' incorporation into democratic life
3. **End of Conflict** — bilateral ceasefire, FARC disarmament, reintegration, and surrender of illegal crops
4. **Solution to the Illicit Drug Problem** — crop substitution, rural development, drug interdiction
5. **Victims** — reparations, truth, justice, and non-repetition
6. **Implementation and Verification**

### Havana as the Negotiating Venue

The formal opening session on November 19, 2012 in Havana was attended by delegations of roughly 30 people on each side. The choice of Cuba as the principal venue was deliberate: Cuba had longstanding relations with the FARC and credibility as a neutral space for Colombian left-wing actors. Norway, which had hosted previous Colombian peace processes, served as the second guarantor. Chile and Venezuela played facilitating roles.

The government's lead negotiator was Humberto de la Calle, a former vice president and constitutional expert. The FARC delegation was led by Iván Márquez, a senior commander and one of the organization's most prominent political intellectuals. The structure was notably different from previous failed Colombian peace processes (1998–2002 Caguán talks under President Andrés Pastrana) in that it was conducted without demilitarized zones granted to the FARC and without interrupting military operations.

## Domestic Political Opposition

Santos's decision to pursue peace talks met fierce resistance from former president Álvaro Uribe, who campaigned vocally against the process from 2012 onward and eventually formed an opposition political movement specifically to challenge the peace accord. Uribe and his allies argued that the framework granted impunity to FARC leaders responsible for war crimes and human rights violations, and that political participation rights for demobilized combatants would allow the FARC to pursue through elections what they had failed to achieve through armed force. This internal opposition ultimately contributed to the rejection of the first peace accord in a national referendum on October 2, 2016 — defeated by a margin of 50.2% to 49.8% — before a revised accord was passed through Congress.

## International Context and Regional Significance

Santos's initiative reflected a broader regional trend toward negotiated conflict resolution. In 2012, Latin America was experiencing a period of relative democratic consolidation, with former guerrilla organizations in El Salvador (FMLN) and Nicaragua (FSLN) having transitioned into legitimate political parties. The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights and the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs had both escalated advocacy for peace in Colombia, citing the humanitarian cost of the ongoing conflict. The European Union, which had designated the FARC a terrorist organization, cautiously welcomed the talks while maintaining its designation.

The economic dimension was also significant: Colombia's ongoing integration into global markets — Santos had concluded a free trade agreement with the United States that took effect in May 2012 — made internal conflict stability increasingly important. Conflict-related disruption to oil infrastructure, pipeline bombings, and rural insecurity constrained investment and rural development, linking peacebuilding directly to Colombia's macroeconomic trajectory. ## Legacy

The peace process launched in November 2012 culminated in the final accord signed on November 24, 2016 in Bogotá, after four years of negotiations and the revised text following the referendum defeat. The FARC's official disarmament was completed by June 2017, with the organization surrendering 8,994 weapons. Santos was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize on October 7, 2016, the prize committee citing his "resolute efforts to bring the country's more than 50-year-long civil war to an end." Despite ongoing implementation challenges — particularly regarding security guarantees for demobilized combatants, rural land reform, and the continued activity of FARC dissidents — the 2016 accord marked the end of the world's then-longest-running insurgency. The 2012 moment of negotiation launch was the foundational act that made this outcome possible.
