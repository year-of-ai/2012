## Summary

Hurricane Sandy formed in the central Caribbean Sea on **22 October 2012** and struck the northeastern United States on the evening of **29 October 2012**, making landfall near Brigantine, New Jersey at approximately **8:00 PM EDT**. At landfall Sandy had sustained winds of around 80 mph, placing it at the lower end of Category 1 intensity, though its enormous size — a tropical-storm-force wind field spanning over 900 miles in diameter — set it apart from comparably-rated storms. Total damage was estimated at approximately **$68.7 billion** (2012 USD), making Sandy the second-costliest hurricane in US history at the time, behind only Hurricane Katrina (2005).

Sandy was responsible for at least **233 deaths** across eight countries from the Caribbean to Canada, including the **147 direct deaths** attributed to the storm by the National Hurricane Center; the United States accounted for the majority of the casualties. New York City and coastal New Jersey bore the brunt of the damage. At The Battery in Lower Manhattan, the storm tide reached a record **14.06 feet above mean lower low water** (a water level of 13.88 feet recorded at 9:24 PM EDT) — the highest ever measured at that gauge, surpassing the previous record of 10.02 feet set by Hurricane Donna in 1960. The storm surge component (the rise above the predicted tide) was about **9.4 feet**. Approximately **43 of New York City's 51 community districts** experienced some degree of flooding.

## Timeline and Track

Sandy's path was unusual for an Atlantic hurricane. After forming near Jamaica, it tracked northward along the US East Coast before making a sharp left turn inland driven by an anomalous blocking high pressure system over Greenland and a deep mid-latitude trough to the west. This left-hook trajectory — combined with a merging extratropical system — caused meteorologists to classify Sandy as a "post-tropical cyclone" just before landfall, though its impacts were indistinguishable from those of a major hurricane.

## Infrastructure Damage

### New York City
The Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) reported that **seven of the ten underwater subway tunnels** linking Manhattan to the outer boroughs were flooded. Service across the entire subway network was suspended, with some lines out of service for weeks. The Hugh L. Carey Tunnel (Brooklyn–Battery Tunnel) and other road tunnels were inundated. LaGuardia Airport sustained significant flooding of runways and terminals.

**Consolidated Edison** shut down power to approximately **1 million customers** in New York City as a precaution before the storm; at peak outage, **8.5 million customers** across the US Northeast were without power, with some areas in New Jersey and on Long Island without electricity for two to three weeks.

The **New York Stock Exchange** closed on **29–30 October** — the first weather-related two-day closure since the **Blizzard of 1888** — halting trading in US equities for consecutive sessions.

### New Jersey and the Mid-Atlantic
Coastal communities including Hoboken, the Rockaways, Seaside Heights, Toms River, and Belmar experienced catastrophic flooding and structural destruction. The famous Seaside Heights boardwalk and amusement structures were largely destroyed. Fuel shortages created lines stretching miles at gas stations across New Jersey and New York in the weeks following landfall, prompting odd-even rationing schemes.

## Political Dimensions

Sandy struck eight days before the **2012 US presidential election**, thrusting disaster response into a high-profile political moment. President Obama declared federal emergencies in affected states and visited New Jersey on **31 October 2012**. New Jersey Governor **Chris Christie** — a prominent Republican surrogate for presidential candidate Mitt Romney — praised Obama's response in terms that led to significant media attention about bipartisan cooperation. The images of Christie and Obama surveying damage together were cited in post-election analyses as contributing to Obama's image of competent leadership in the final week of the [2012 US presidential election]({{ '/news/history-politics/2012-us-presidential-election/' | relative_url }}).

FEMA's response under Administrator Craig Fugate was broadly regarded as more effective than the federal response to Hurricane Katrina, reflecting organizational reforms implemented following the Katrina After-Action Review.

## Occupy Sandy and Civil Society Response

One of the notable social consequences of Hurricane Sandy was the emergence of **Occupy Sandy**, a grassroots mutual-aid network coordinated largely by veterans of the [Occupy Wall Street movement]({{ '/news/society-economics/occupy-wall-street-2012/' | relative_url }}), which had been active in 2011–12. Occupy Sandy established distribution hubs at churches and community centers in the Rockaways, Red Hook (Brooklyn), Staten Island's North Shore, and coastal New Jersey. Volunteers coordinated logistics via social media and improvised supply chains at a speed that outpaced official distribution networks in the immediate post-landfall period.

The network raised millions of dollars in donations and distributed food, water, clothing, and emergency supplies to hundreds of thousands of affected residents. Its methods — decentralized, relying on existing social relationships and direct community engagement — attracted academic and policy attention as a model of disaster mutual aid. For many participants, Occupy Sandy represented a practical application of the horizontal organizing principles developed during the Occupy encampments of 2011.

## Climate Policy Implications

Sandy accelerated mainstream policy debate about urban coastal resilience and the relationship between climate change and extreme weather. In its aftermath:

- New York Governor Andrew Cuomo established a commission on climate adaptation, leading to the **NY Rising** program and eventual planning for large-scale coastal protection projects.
- The federal government's **Hurricane Sandy Rebuilding Task Force** coordinated a multi-agency response and issued a comprehensive rebuilding strategy in 2013.
- Scientists and policy advocates cited Sandy as a "teachable moment" for communicating climate risk to urban populations, noting that rising sea levels would increase the frequency of Sandy-level surge events over the coming decades.

Sandy became a recurring reference point in US climate policy discussions throughout the 2010s, cited in debates over the infrastructure bill, coastal zone management, and federal flood insurance reform.

## Significance

Hurricane Sandy's legacy encompasses multiple dimensions: the immediate human and economic toll; the political dynamics of a major disaster coinciding with a presidential election; the emergence of a new civic mutual-aid model out of the Occupy movement's organizational infrastructure; and the lasting influence on coastal resilience policy. The storm demonstrated both the vulnerability of highly interconnected urban infrastructure to extreme weather and the capacity for rapid civil-society organization in the absence of adequate state response.
